Drifting Gaze - 1

Scanned digital image of negative films

2022

This is an ongoing project for my graduation thesis, and the final stage would be an installation. I hope to capture, in this series of works, how vision moves around the mundane corners of modern urban areas.

Spaces in urban areas are mostly function-based, and people interact with those space with not visual interest but bodily functions. Materials and design of those spaces are seemingly repetitive, and they are to be passed by rather than gazed at, yet they exists in sides of our vision.

In drifting gaze I attempt to capture those corners of city and of vision in away that no typical “main role” exist in the images.




Drifting Gaze - 2

(Collage) Digital image

2022

This part of work is created with by stacking images from left and right lenses taken by an old 6 by 6 stereo film camera.

This series was focused on exploring cognitical characteristics of human vision. Typically, the stereo effect of human eyes and the way our brain combines images gained in both eyes --- something that, due to their point-projection nature, most cameras cannot capture.

I realised that images perceived are mostly not singular - only seemingly singular at the center of vision. Most parts in peripheral vision are rather seen in a form of collage, and, the cen- tral vision shifts constantly. Exsistence of such a form of collage plays a role in perception of space, while also creates aethetical experience of ambiguity, of not perceiving clearly.



Foggy, Sometimes Sunny

Digital image

2022

In April of 2022, Shanghai of China entered a prolonging period of lockdown. It wasn’t claimed to be lasting for long, and citizens en- tered the period unprepared.

As days passed, although procedures to ensure certain level of life necessities were gradually being taken, breaking down of supply chain, medical resources and overall changes in ev- eryday lives are putting people’s state of minds at danger. Deaths, confrontations, and hugely supressed emotions accumulated, and a online rally of spreading the information against cen- sorship took place. I thought I need to react and go to the front line as a photographer, yet that was physically impossible.

On April 25th I decided that I could at least ex- perience the bodily sensations of volunteers in Shanghai, and see how that influence myself. So I started to go anywhere in a set of personal pro- tective equipment (PPE) from China, the same equipment of some health workers.

PPE imediately changed the way I interact with the world, and I tried to document those chang- es through diary and photographs (my friends also helped to document me). I felt isolated from the outer world, even from normal access of air and light. The way people interacte with me also changed vastly.

I took off the PPE on the day lockdown ended

on 30th of June. People in Shanghai were told that the lockdown was pure voluntarily, not by government orders.

Foggy, Sometimes Sunny is a small by-product of this process, and it consists of photos that I took across the foggy eyepiece caused by my breath and sweating, and photos my friends took of me. I hope to avoid overly political ex- pressions, and try to break the stereotypes con- nected to the symbolic look of PPE.

Ongoing Project: People, Time, Space

Digital Images 2021~2022

In this experimental project, I use a 360° camera to document how humans interact with the surrounding space.

I take timelapse video data and calculate them by deviation or mean algorithms in Photoshop, leaving traces of people (mostly myself) in the surrounding\ environment. Resulting photographs can also be viewed in 3D photo viewers.

It started as a way of documenting myself staying at home and wearing masks outside during Covid times. I place the camera on tables around me when I am in a room or Cafe and hold the cam- era with a selfie stick in front of me when moving around. In the latter case, the space is dragged and reformed or even wiped out by my movements.


Approaching (We cannot stop at photos anymore)

(Installation) Screen, Laptop, Depth-sensing Camera 2021

*Please refer to video named Approaching for installation look Photography is facing unparrelleled boom in our age, with almost everybody having easy access of photographical equipments and

sharing platforms at hand. However, photography is also deminished in a way that they are mostly cosumed on SNS platforms, with simple thumb swipes bringing new waves of information.

“Approaching” is an attempt to change the common thumb wiping motion of viewing images from smartphone to bodily movements.



Looking back, “Approaching” and “People, Time, Space” are two typ- ically important works for me. I was interested in the relationship be- tween human body and space around them before, yet I was not clearly implementing them in my personal works.

Those two series were both made after my first few attempts of pho- tographical installations, where I realised the importance of bodily movements, and the relationship that viewers holds to images.

I began considering one-dimentional media in their roles in space,

and started to exlpore the process not only in installations but also in overall themes of my works.

In my graduation thesis, though I plan to take a more traditional pho- tographical approach, I think of photography making differently, and began exploring the bodily sides of vision in our perception, as well as the way human body interacts with the surrounding environment.

I hope to continue this theme in with more varied media and possi- bilities in the future.

Ome Yamamizu Tensenzu

(Installation) Inkjet Print, Aluminum Board 2021


This is a project done during Area Study class in Ome City. Ome is like the entrance from the urban land- scape to nature, where trails for hik- ers and anglers start. The landscape is seemingly natural yet primarily de- signed and reshaped by humans and influenced by human activity. Even the woods are mostly replanted in the last century. I used a laser projector to create straight lines in the steep “entrance” to the Tama river and Mt. Odake area, attempting to reveal how a surface would have sliced the landscape in and show that they were strictly measured and reshaped for the convenience of human activities. I project lights onto the landscape, capture the way that light look, and then walk towards direction of the light, to capture the startpoint of the projection.

Project: Dropped Universe

Digital Photography, Video (2 min 37 seconds) 2020

*Please refer to uploaded video for complete work Dropped Universe is a small project taken at the crossroads near my apartment.

The cross-section was paved with asphalt and mixed glass fragments, reflecting lights and warning vehicles passing by.

I found the surface typically fascinating, as it creates the sensation of looking at a clear night sky, which is almost impossible in metropolitans such as Tokyo, where light pollution overwhelms lights from distant stars at night.

This work has a hypothetical background of two people dropping their shopping of fruits and nuts and finding the universe below their feet.

I tried to make the photography works deceiving and the video silly (refering to Charles Chapper- lin Films in a way) to create a certain sensation of satire. It is not towards the viewer, nor the characters in the film, but the whole sense of greatness in space exploration. While it is human nature to explore the unknown, space imagery has been, since after WWII, symbolical to great political and economic powers. The possibility of sensing the beauty and greatness of the universe through a minor incident of daily activity is a weirdly intriguing plot twist for me.



Modeled Nightscape

Photobook 2019~2021


Modeled Nightscape is based on the similar- ities and differences of urban nightscapes to 3D rendering.

Urban landscapes are largely designed and reconstructed by humans, and especially at night, human light sources even adds to such an effect.

Spending hours wandering around Tokyo suburbs at night since first grade in college, at times I get submerged in a sensation of not knowing if the world around me is real or simulated --- especially when they are folded into one-dimentional photography, that line gets even more blurry.




Pending Project: Vanishing Factories

Digital Photography 2019

This is a project that I started during the summer vacation of 2019 and never got to continue to due to Covid restrictions.

I was born and raised until seven at a power plant in a rural area of China in Shanxi province. It was one of the communities quite identi- cal to communist countries, where young people used to graduate from schools and get appointed to a designated site of planned develop- ment, go and settle in the place while building and working there. Most industrial areas are built under this societal structure.

Some of those sites diminished during China’s “Reforming and Opening” movement. Currently, due to the change of policy concerning typically environmental issues, another wave of disabling of old industrial structures is taking place.

Unlike in most countries, in China, those structures also are the community of workers and their families, which means people are more tied to these sites geographically and emotionally. I, too, am somewhat bound to those places.

I hope to be able to get back to this project and make a documentary series with photos, found photos, and interviews of residents.
